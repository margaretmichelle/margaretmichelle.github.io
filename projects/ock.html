Mister OCK

(Octopus Control Kinematics)

| 2025 |

Overview:

While interning at Formlabs, I participated in a hackathon sponsored by the company and developed a soft robotics octopus arm with fellow intern, Vishesh Garg. Soft robotics has become an increasingly prominent field of study motivated by the challenges conventional rigid robots face interacting with the environment, particularly with gripping complex or fragile objects. My teammate and I wanted to design an arm to be printed using Formlabs' flexible resins that can grip objects and look cool while doing it. In a weekend, we achieved simultaneous control of two 1-DOF octopus arms capable of handling objects, and underactuated control of a 2-DOF octopus arm capable of looking cool.

Soft robotics SolidWorks MATLAB Rapid prototyping Compliant mechanism design SLA 3D printing

My Contributions:

  • Modeled octopus arms in SolidWorks to be printed on Form 4Ls
  • Iterated over 15+ arms by:
    • Simulating curl motion and final position in MATLAB to optimize joint geometry and link lengths, translating that to SolidWorks design
    • Rapidly printing and evaluating versions of arms with different materials and geometries to converge upon a satisfactory spiral
  • Redesigned the 1-DOF arm into a 2-DOF system, including a complete reconfiguration of the arm, mount, and pulleys
  • Assembled arm, motor, mount, pulley, and fishing line system developing thread tensioning and fastening technique using ferrules


Project Details:

The final 1-DOF octopus arm system consists of two compliant arms bolted to a 3D-printed mounting structure, which also houses two stepper motors driving pulley mechanisms. Fishing line tendons were routed along both sides of each arm and wrapped around the corresponding pulley in opposing directions. Rotating the pulley increases tension in one tendon while releasing the opposing tendon simultaneously to produce controlled curling of the arm. The system uses two stepper motor drivers powered by a 24 V supply, and is controlled with an Arduino Mega running a script to manually drive the motors. The 2-DOF system has a similar architecture except tendons were routed through each face of the arm, with tendons from opposing faces connected to the same pulley.

Drawing inspiration from a similar design, I modeled the octopus arm in SolidWorks with the requirement that it needed to be 3D printed on an SLA printer. To determine the chamfer angles of the links as well link lengths, I created a simple simulation of the geometry in MATLAB to see how the arm looked as it closed. This informed the final design of the arm.

%% PARAMETERS
N_links = 16;

dtheta = deg2rad(0.3);

base_w = 16;
base_angle = deg2rad(90);
l1_w = 14;
l1_angle = deg2rad(80);

% Joints
j_angles_deg = [10 10 20 20 30 30 30 30 30 30 30 30 30 30 30 30 0];
j_angles = deg2rad(j_angles_deg);
j_dist = [0 20 38 55 71 86 100 113 126 138 149 159 169 178 186 194 200] * 1;

% Links
l_angles_deg = [85 85 80 80 80 80 80 80 80 80 80 80 80 80 80 80];
l_angles = deg2rad(l_angles_deg);
l_widths = [14 13 12 11.4 10.7 10 9.4 8.9 8.3 7.8 7.3 6.9 6.4 6. 5.7 5.2];

% Base
base_width = 16;

%% Define links and joints
% Joint points
j_pts = [zeros(1, N_links+1); j_dist(1:N_links+1)];

% Base points
base_pts = [base_width base_width; -48 0];

% Link points
l_pts1 = zeros(2, N_links); % bottom points of link edge
l_pts2 = zeros(2, N_links); % top points of link edge

for link = 1:N_links
    w = l_widths(link);
    joint = j_dist(link);
    if link == 1
        angle_bottom = pi/2 - j_angles(link) - pi/2;
    else
        angle_bottom = pi/2 - j_angles(link) - l_angles(link - 1);
    end
    angle_top = pi/2 - l_angles(link);

    l_pts1(1, link) = w;
    l_pts1(2, link) = joint - w * tan(angle_bottom);

    l_pts2(1, link) = w;
    l_pts2(2, link) = j_dist(link+1) - abs(w * tan(angle_top));
end

% Left side
l_pts1_L = [-l_pts1(1,:); l_pts1(2,:)];
l_pts2_L = [-l_pts2(1,:); l_pts2(2,:)];


%% Helper functions
R = @(P,th,p) ([cos(th) -sin(th); sin(th) cos(th)] * (P - p)) + p;


function P = points_in_order(joints, links_1, links_2)
    n = size(links_1,2);

    P = zeros(2, 4*n);

    P(:, 1:4:end) = joints(:,1);
    P(:, 2:4:end) = links_1;
    P(:, 3:4:end) = links_2;
    P(:, 4:4:end) = joints(:,2);

end

%% Simulation loop
figure; hold on; axis equal; grid off;
xlabel('X'); ylabel('Y');
title('Mister Ock Simulation');


for th = 0:dtheta:max(j_angles)
    cla;
    P = [base_pts(:,1) base_pts(:,2) j_pts(:,1)];
    plot(P(1,:), P(2,:), 'k', 'LineWidth', 2);
    P = [-P(1,:); P(2,:)];
    plot(P(1,:), P(2,:), 'k', 'LineWidth', 2);

    for joint = 1:N_links
        if th+deg2rad(2) < j_angles(joint)
            j_pts(:,joint:end) = R(j_pts(:,joint:end),-dtheta,j_pts(:,joint));
            l_pts1(:,joint:end) = R(l_pts1(:,joint:end),-dtheta,j_pts(:,joint));
            l_pts2(:,joint:end) = R(l_pts2(:,joint:end),-dtheta,j_pts(:,joint));

            l_pts1_L(:,joint:end) = R(l_pts1_L(:,joint:end),-dtheta,j_pts(:,joint));
            l_pts2_L(:,joint:end) = R(l_pts2_L(:,joint:end),-dtheta,j_pts(:,joint));

        end
        P = points_in_order(j_pts(:,joint:joint+1), l_pts1(:,joint), l_pts2(:,joint));
        plot(P(1,:), P(2,:), 'k', 'LineWidth', 2);

        P = points_in_order(j_pts(:,joint:joint+1), l_pts1_L(:,joint), l_pts2_L(:,joint));
        plot(P(1,:), P(2,:), 'k', 'LineWidth', 2);

        plot(j_pts(1,joint), j_pts(2,joint),'bo','MarkerFaceColor','b', 'MarkerSize', 2);
    end
    drawnow;
    pause(0.02);
end

A parameter not accounted for in the MATLAB simulation, was the thickness of the joints which dictates the force required to close each gap and therefore the order in which the gaps between the links close. We determined the joint thicknesses through a trial and error process of rapid iteration and evaluation. We also took advantage of the multiple printers available to us to compare Flexible 80A and Elastic 50A resins. We found that Elastic 50A was not stiff enough so we went with the material with the higher Shore A hardness: Flexible 80A. We also rapidly prototyped pulley designs varying diameters, groove angles, and materials.

Our primary failure mode within the system was pulley failure. We broke a large number of pulley shafts due to combined bending and torsional loads amplified by the stress concentrations where the shaft exits the motor housing and at the shaftâ€“hub (chamfer) transition. A large contributor to these failures was the flawed, though simple, opposing-wrap design because the fishing line does not simultaneously tension and slack perfectly in practice. This led to increased tendon forces and radial loading as well as a higher likelihood of snagging or overtensioning with repeated cycles. Additionally, the pulleys were coupled to the motors via unsupported cantilevered shafts and thus experienced significant bending moments; adding an outboard bearing would have reduced shaft bending and improved pulley lifetime. Finally, our pulleys were printed in Rigid 10K resin for its high stiffness, but this material is brittle. Printing the pulleys out of a more ductile material such as Tough or Durable would have increased pulley survivability, albeit with stiffness trade-offs.
