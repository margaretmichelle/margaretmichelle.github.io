Overview

POLARIS is a fully autonomous sailboat designed by students to collect data in the Pacific Ocean. I lead the design and manufacturing of the hull, keel, and cradle. We used Maxsurf to analyze and optimize hydrostatic and hydrodymanic properties, and SolidWorks and ANSYS to characterize her structure and select appropriate materials. In Fall 2024, we completed the manufacturing of her hull involving large-scale carbon fibre layups over a wood core. Learn more about POLARIS here.


Design

Maxsurf

We designed our hull using Maxsurf Naval Architecture Software (Modeler, Stability, and Resistance). The shape was loosely inspired by Saildrone's USVs, heavily modified to fit our size and manufacturing requirements, and optimized to work with the rest of the sailboat system. The displacement was designed to hold all of our components (sail, rudder, keel, electronics, etc.) as well as the weight of the hull itself while floating with our set waterline. The length, shape, and waterline were iterated to obtain a resistance that works with the lift generated by our rigid wingsail at our design speed. The righting moment, dictated by the shape of the hull and its waterline, and moment balance was analyzed to design a suitable bulb keel. Further, the hull was adjusted to improve manufacturability. All these properties are unmistakably related and required an iterative design cycle, clear design constraints and criteria, and careful trade-off analysis.


Ansys

Our next step was determining the material and internal structure known as bulkheads to strengthen and partition the hull. One of my members and I used Ansys Static Structural to examine the stresses and deformation resulting from loads including

  • Hydrostatic forces
  • Hydrodynamic forces
  • Lift, drag, and weight from the sail
  • Lift, drag, and weight from the rudder
  • Lift, drag, and weight from the keel
We used Ansys Composite PrepPost (ACP) to model our composite sandwich panels, settling on a marine-grade plywood core for the hull and a PVC foam core for the deck after a WDM analysis to balance weight, cost, and strength among other criteria. A carbon fibre skin was chosen due to availability and experience as our last two vessels utilized carbon fibre.

SolidWorks

The hull was transferred into SolidWorks where we finished the design, added sensors, hatches, electrical enclosures, and integrated with the sail and rudder assemblies. The keel was designed in SolidWorks based on our previously determined parameters while creating the hull. We use proper naming convention and version control tables to keep track of all the contributions from our many members, a system I created and implemented. We are currently still working on finalizing all intgration details as well as desiging a cradle for land transportation and launch of the sailboat with SolidWorks.


Manufacturing

Over two months during our winter school term, we manufactured our hull and bulkheads, and began to build the keel (big shoutout to AMPEL for letting us use their space). I organized sessions of up to 20 members performing large carbon fibre hand layups with subsequent vacuum bagging, wood cutting and assembly, and fairing sessions to level and smooth the exterior surface of the vessel, just to name a few of our tasks. Though many lessons were learned, the manufacturing sprint was a great success.
